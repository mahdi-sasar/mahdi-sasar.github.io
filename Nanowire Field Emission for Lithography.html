Nanowire Field Emission for Lithography

I have done some interesting work in the past using field emission from ZnO nanowires and carbon nanotubes (CNTs) to clear away any photoresist where the nanostructure is present. Of course, the main idea belongs to my past PhD supervisor, when he himself was a PhD student. However, we both made the mistake of using standard photolithography photoresists to do these experiments. In fact, we should have used e-beam lithography resins (or X-ray lithography resins, which cure by the same principles as e-beam, using secondary electron emissions after X-ray irradiation). E-beam resins cure due to the polymerization (or the inverse of this depending on whether we are talking about negative or positive resists) started by the impacting electron. So, when we have a NiO thin film, I can grow a sparse array of ZnO nanorods on it, while I can also enhance the verticality by the idea here: [[Vertical ZnO Hydrothermal Growth Idea]]. Next, an e-beam resist (such as PMMA) can be used to coat the surface of this layer. Then by pressing an anode to the top, we can force the ZnO nanowires to emit electrons, acting basically as cold cathodes. Then, the emitted electrons will cure the resin where the nanorods are and when washed with the developer, we will have a perfect lithography process. Everywhere is covered, but the tips of the nanorods. Incidentally, this workflow can also be used to create UV LED from the NiO/ZnO junction: [[UV LED from NiO-ZnO junction]].

This paper also has an innovative approach to extracting (cold cathode) field emission from ZnO tetrapods. The overall geometry of the cathode, anode and gate were interesting and might be useful for my future device architecture.

Another important thing to note is that PMMA is a positive tone resist: wherever is exposed to electron beams is more easily dissolved. This makes it perfect for our current applications. Currently, ASML’s fantastic design of EUV (see [[ASML’s Extreme UV Laser]]) has a single handed monopoly on the market. They have done a remarkable job and the final device, with an operating wavelength of 13.5 nm, is a testament to decades of R&D. X-rays were always present in these years, but their application has been very limited. Much more limited than the current benchmark of the semiconductor industry of DUV lithography (deep UV lithography) which uses excimer lasers. Main reason behind this is that most x-ray lithography relies on x-ray emission from synchrotrons, and they are very expensive and have a large footprint. Therefore, x-ray lithography doesn’t seem to have been widely adopted for mass production in the semiconductor industry. Challenges of x-ray lithography and their resolution in my paradigm: 1. Infrastructure: X-ray lithography requires the availability of synchrotron radiation or X-ray laser sources, which are expensive and not easily accessible. >Solution: >My method of cold cathode x-ray production removes the need for massive synchrotrons and costly equipment. Throughput: The throughput of X-ray lithography has historically been lower compared to other lithography techniques, making it less suitable for high-volume production. >Solution: >The throughput stands on the same level as the EUV and DUV. The problem is in steering the focused light spot on the resist layer. The EUV animations show that the XY scan happen by a mechanical XY stage. These stages are probably high quality high precision piezo actuators, and they must be used for these purposes as well. 1. Cost: The infrastructure and equipment costs associated with X-ray lithography have been a significant barrier to its widespread adoption. >Solution: >The cost of cold cathode designs are very low and they van be quickly replaced in case they sustain any damage.
